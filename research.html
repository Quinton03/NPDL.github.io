Areas of Research

01

Development and plasticity in Deafness and blindness

Large sections of the primate cortex are dedicated to sensory input, but what happens when these parts of the brain do not receive "species typical input" during development? Are these areas repurposed to support non-canonical cognitive functions, and are these functions similar to vision or audition? We examine "visual" and "auditory" cortex plasticity in blindness and deafness to investigate this question.

In blindness, we find that "visual" areas become involved in higher-cognitive functions including language, mathematical reasoning, and non-verbal executive control. Furthermore, these functions each recruit different parts of the "visual" cortex. These examinations provide insights into the mechanisms that determine cortical specialization in humans.

02

Language across modalities

Complex language is a hallmark of human cognition. Decades of prior research have studied the brain's ability to support language in its spoken and written modalities, but little is understood regarding the development of linguistic ability when either is unavailable. In these cases, people are able to adapt by using other senses to facilitate communication.

Our lab studies the brains of developing and proficient signers and braille-readers to identify both modality-invariant and modality-specific aspects of language development using fMRI and TMS.

03

Cultural expertise

Many of our cognitive capabilities and the cortical structures on which they depend were developed over evolutionary timescales. This includes memory, sensation, motility, and social reasoning. However, a core feature of intelligence is adaptability. Humans in particular display an exceptional capacity to develop culturally relevant skills (like coding or medicine) more quickly than new cortical architecture could evolve to support them. So, how does the brain achieve expertise so rapidly?

We study this capacity using fMRI, observing the brain as people become proficient and reach expertise in cultural skills. How much does the brain rely on related specialized structures to adapt to new demands? Do new skills displace old skills in the cortex? Our analyses aim to provide answers to these questions.

04

Concepts and experience

Our language, behavior, and our internal models of the world are founded on our ability to form concepts; but how do we build these concepts through our experience and how are they represented in the brain? Our lab is primarily focused on acquisition: how much of our conceptual knowledge is innate, how much is derived from direct sensory experience, and how much can be learned from language alone?

We investigate these questions with the help of congenitally blind individuals using fMRI and behavioral measures. Even without direct experience, blind individuals are able to describe visual concepts accurately and use them predictively.
